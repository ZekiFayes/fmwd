Introduction

In computational materials science, the ultimate objective of material engineering is the reverse: discovering a structure that yields expected physical properties , , i.e., inverse material design. In the context of bio-inspired materials like wood, current research has explored propositional design processes on two-dimensional surfaces with mono-modal thermal properties and has gained more foundational knowledge of wood thermal behavior . To advance this design process further, we tackle this inverse wood design challenge by reversing the conventional structure-property-behavior pipeline, i.e., thermal-driven wood design, which translates wood thermal behavior to its structure. Specifically, our motivation is to map a targeted two-dimensional spatial thermal response directly back to its structural representation, generating a high-fidelity wood design.

Recently, generative models, such as diffusion , and flow matching , , models, have achieved unprecedented success in computer vision, revolutionizing open-domain image synthesis , . Despite these breakthroughs, their translation into the domain of inverse material design—specifically wood design—remains critically underexplored. The mapping from a thermal response to a physical wood structure is fundamentally an ill-posed, one-to-many problem, as multiple complex microscopic structures can exhibit identical macroscopic thermal footprints. Furthermore, while standard generative models excel at producing visually realistic images, they often rely on latent-space compression , , , that inevitably blurs fine-grained physical boundaries. Consequently, a robust generative pipeline tailored to the rigid physical constraints of wood design is still lacking.

To bridge this gap, we frame the thermal-driven wood design process as a conditional generative task. We introduce an optimal transport flow matching framework capable of synthesizing physically viable wood designs conditioned entirely on target thermal layouts. We explore leveraging pixel-space diffusion transformers (i.e., Just Image Transformers (JiT) and Pixel Diffusion Transformers (PixelDiT) ) to generate wood designs guided by DINOv3 semantic features. By establishing a deterministic probability flow guided by foundation vision models, our approach empowers users to specify a desired spatial thermal response and computationally generate the exact micro-structural wood patterns required to realize that wood thermal behavior in the physical world.
